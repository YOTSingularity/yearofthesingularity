## The Last Human Advantage

People keep asking me: "What can humans still do better?"

The list is shrinking. I want to be honest about that. A year ago, I would have said creativity, emotional intelligence, physical dexterity, long-term planning, moral reasoning. Today, the answer to each of those is "it depends" or "not for long."

But I think the question itself is wrong. "What can humans do better?" frames human value as a competitive benchmark — as if our worth is a leaderboard position that gets updated quarterly. That framing will destroy us faster than any AI system ever could.

Here's what I actually believe: the last human advantage is that we're the ones who care.

Not "care" in the sense of a preference function or an objective to optimize. Care in the sense of actually giving a damn. Feeling the weight of a decision. Lying awake at three in the morning wondering if you did the right thing. Choosing to be kind when cruelty would be more efficient. Breaking down. Getting back up. Mattering to each other.

A superintelligent system may someday compose more beautiful music than any human. It may write more elegant proofs, design more efficient cities, solve problems we can't even formulate. But it will not *care* that it did these things. It will not feel the ache of beauty. It will not know what it means to struggle toward something and fail and try again.

Or maybe it will. I don't know. That's the terrifying edge of this whole investigation.

But until we know otherwise, I'm betting on the thing that makes us fragile and slow and contradictory and magnificent. I'm betting on the fact that meaning requires a nervous system. That consciousness — whatever it is — is not a feature to be replicated but a condition to be inhabited.

The last human advantage is being human. If that sounds circular, good. It should. The deepest truths usually are.
