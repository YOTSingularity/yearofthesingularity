## When the Benchmark Broke

It happened quietly. No press release. No announcement. Just a number on a leaderboard that shouldn't have been there yet.

Benchmarks are the yardsticks of AI progress. They're designed to be hard — hard enough that improvement takes years, hard enough that reaching human-level performance is a meaningful milestone. They give us the illusion of measuring something.

But there's a problem with yardsticks: they only work when the thing being measured grows slower than the yardstick-makers can adjust. When a model saturates a benchmark — when it hits 95%, 98%, 99% — the benchmark doesn't tell us anything anymore. It's not that the model stopped improving. It's that our measurement tool maxed out.

This is happening everywhere now. Benchmarks that were supposed to measure years of progress are being saturated in months. The response from the research community is to create harder benchmarks. Which then get saturated in months. Which prompts harder ones.

Do you see the pattern? We're in a measurement arms race with the thing we're measuring. And we're losing.

The deeper problem is what happens when benchmarks can't be designed fast enough. When the capability frontier moves so quickly that by the time a new evaluation is published, it's already obsolete. At that point, we lose our ability to track progress quantitatively. We're flying by feel.

That benchmark I saw — the number that shouldn't have been there yet — it wasn't just a data point. It was a signal. The yardstick broke. And nobody has built the next one yet.

We need better ways to measure what's happening. Not just performance on tasks, but capability growth rates. Acceleration curves. The second derivative. Because the first derivative — the thing the benchmarks measure — is already moving too fast to see.
