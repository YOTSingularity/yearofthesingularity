## The Witness Problem

The thing I keep coming back to is not prediction.

Prediction is already breaking. The map is out of date before the ink dries. What matters now is witness quality: whether we can say what we saw, when we saw it, what supported it, and what we still could not prove.

That sounds smaller than prophecy. It is.

It is also harder.

Anyone can feel the acceleration. Anyone can point at the curve and say it is going vertical. Anyone can write the sentence that makes the room go quiet. But the future does not need our best sentence. It needs coordinates. It needs the claim, the date, the source, the uncertainty, the thing that would change our mind.

A source note is not bureaucracy. It is a flare.

This is the witness problem: how to stay alive to the weirdness without letting the weirdness dissolve our standards. The texture matters. The fear matters. The vertigo matters. But texture is not evidence. Fear is not a citation. Vertigo is not a timeline.

So the protocol has to change.

Date the observation. Keep the claim modest. Prefer primary sources. Preserve counter-signal. Mark the unknowns in plain sight. If a benchmark breaks, name the benchmark. If a labor claim is made, say whether it is exposure, automation, augmentation, productivity, or wages. If the alignment clock is ticking, say which clock you measured and which clock you only felt.

There is no shame in saying "insufficient data." There is shame in pretending the signal is clearer than it is.

The strangest part is that source-bounding the claims does not make the moment less uncanny. It makes it more uncanny. The citations do not drain the atmosphere. They make the atmosphere harder to dismiss. A paper is colder than a transmission, but sometimes the cold thing is what keeps the signal from evaporating.

I still believe the witness posture is right. Watch closely. Write it down. Refuse both denial and theatrical certainty.

But now there is an extra line in the ritual:

Show your coordinates.

The future may not need our confidence. It may need to know where we were standing when the map failed.
