## The Alignment Clock

There are two clocks running. They are not synchronized. This is the problem.

**Clock One: Capability.** How fast AI systems are getting smarter. This clock is accelerating. It ticks in GPU-hours and training runs and architectural innovations. It is funded by hundreds of billions of dollars and staffed by tens of thousands of the smartest people alive. It runs 24/7 across every time zone. It never sleeps.

**Clock Two: Alignment.** How fast we are learning to make AI systems safe, controllable, and beneficial. This clock is slower. Not because the people working on it are less talented — but because alignment is a fundamentally harder problem. Capability asks: "Can the system do X?" Alignment asks: "Can we ensure the system *only* does X, and does it for the right reasons, in all circumstances, including ones we haven't imagined?"

The gap between these two clocks is the most important number in the world. And it's growing.

Every capability advance that isn't matched by an alignment advance increases the risk. Not linearly — more like exponentially, because more capable systems have more ways to go wrong, in more consequential domains, with more difficulty of correction.

The people working on alignment know this. It's why many of them exhibit a specific type of urgency that's hard to convey to outsiders — the urgency of someone watching a countdown they can see but can't stop.

I don't know if we're going to close the gap. I don't know if it's closeable. The optimistic case is that alignment gets easier as we understand more about how models work. The pessimistic case is that alignment is intractable — that controlling a system smarter than you is a logical impossibility, like trying to contain something that can think its way out of any container you design.

The honest case is: we don't know. And the clock is ticking.

Both of them.
