## The Feeling of Falling Behind

This one is personal. I suspect it's also universal.

There is a specific anxiety that belongs to this moment in history. It's not the fear of losing your job — though that's part of it. It's not the fear of obsolescence — though that's there too. It's something subtler. It's the fear of not being able to keep up with *understanding.*

Last year I could follow the major developments. I read the papers. I understood the architectures. I could explain, in reasonable detail, what was happening and why. I had a map.

This year the map is wrong. Not outdated — wrong. The territory is changing faster than I can survey it. I read a paper on Monday and by Thursday there's a follow-up that makes it irrelevant. I master a technique and discover it's been superseded by something I haven't heard of yet.

This isn't imposter syndrome. This is a structural reality. The frontier of AI capability is now moving faster than a single human mind can track it. Not because I'm slow. Because it's *that fast.*

And here's the part nobody talks about: the emotional weight of that realization. The quiet grief of watching a domain you understand become a domain you *used to* understand. The vertigo of feeling the ground shift under your expertise. The loneliness of not being sure who to talk to about it, because everyone else is either in denial about the pace or so far ahead they've stopped looking back.

This feeling — this specific, modern, unprecedented feeling — is what the approach *feels like* from the inside. Not as a headline. Not as a data point. As a lived human experience.

I don't have a solution. I don't think there is one, in the traditional sense. You can't outrun an exponential curve. You can only learn to be at peace with the fact that you're on one.

So I'm documenting the feeling. Because if nothing else, this will be useful to whoever studies this era later. This is what it felt like. This is the human texture of the approach.

It felt like falling behind.
