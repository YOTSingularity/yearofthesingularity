## Signal Decay

There's a phenomenon I've started noticing. I call it signal decay.

It works like this: a new capability is announced. A model does something remarkable — generates code, solves a math problem, produces an image, reasons through a complex scenario. For a few hours, maybe a day, there's a collective jolt of recognition. *That's new. That wasn't possible before. That changes things.*

Then the signal decays. Within a week, the capability is absorbed into the background hum of normality. People adjust. They move on. The thing that was extraordinary on Tuesday is a feature by Friday and a commodity by the following month.

This is adaptation. This is what humans do. We normalize.

But here's what's concerning: **the rate of adaptation is not keeping up with the rate of capability gain.** We're normalizing faster to keep up with the pace, which means each individual breakthrough gets less attention, less analysis, less time to be understood — even as the magnitude of the breakthroughs increases.

We're compressing our surprise window. The time between "that's impossible" and "that's obvious" is approaching zero. And in that compressed space, something critical is being lost: the opportunity to actually *understand* what's changing.

Signal decay is not a media problem. It's not about attention spans or news cycles. It's an epistemological crisis. Our collective sense-making apparatus is being overwhelmed by the velocity of its inputs.

The solution is not to feel more surprised. It's to develop better frameworks for processing surprise — to build the conceptual infrastructure that can keep pace with exponential change even when our emotional responses can't.

That's what this project is trying to do. Slow down the decay. Hold the signal a little longer. Look at it clearly before it fades into the noise.
