## The geometry of acceleration

Exponential growth has a cruel optical illusion built into it. For most of the curve, it looks like a flat line. Then it looks like a gentle slope. Then, in what feels like an instant, it goes vertical. The thing about exponential curves is that every point on the curve *feels* like the early part — until it doesn't.

We believe we are exiting the gentle slope.

<br>

## Five convergences

The approach is not a single phenomenon. It is at least five simultaneous developments colliding in the same decade:

### 1. The scaling convergence

We now understand, with mathematical precision, how model capability improves as a function of compute, data, and parameters. These are not diminishing returns. They are *power laws* — smooth, predictable, relentless. Every order of magnitude in compute buys a quantifiable improvement in capability. And compute is growing exponentially.

The implication is staggering: we can *forecast* capability improvements years in advance, just by looking at hardware roadmaps. The models of 2028 are not mysterious. They are the logical extension of curves we can already plot. The only uncertainty is whether anything interrupts the curve.

Nothing has yet.

### 2. The emergence convergence

As models scale, they develop capabilities that were never explicitly trained. A language model trained on next-token prediction learns to do arithmetic, to reason about physics, to write poetry, to debug code. Nobody programmed these abilities. They *emerged* from scale.

This is arguably the most unsettling observation in modern AI research: we don't fully understand why scale produces these capabilities. We have theories. We have empirical observations. But we don't have a first-principles explanation for why training a system to predict the next word creates something that can reason.

We are building minds we don't fully comprehend. And they're getting more capable every quarter.

### 3. The recursive convergence

AI systems are now active participants in AI research. Models help write code. Models help analyze results. Models help generate hypotheses. The distance between "tool used by researchers" and "researcher" is narrowing.

The endpoint of this trajectory is an AI system that can fully automate the AI research pipeline — from identifying promising research directions, to designing experiments, to running them, to analyzing results, to implementing improvements. When that loop closes, the rate of AI progress decouples from human cognitive speed.

This is what "recursive self-improvement" means in practice. Not a science fiction scenario. An engineering trajectory we are already on.

### 4. The agency convergence

Models are developing the ability to act in the world. Not just generate text — but browse the internet, write and execute code, use software tools, manage multi-step projects, and operate with decreasing levels of human oversight.

Each new capability in the agency stack — planning, tool use, error correction, goal decomposition — brings us closer to systems that can pursue objectives autonomously. The gap between "assistant that helps you do things" and "agent that does things on your behalf" is closing.

### 5. The understanding convergence

Perhaps most profoundly, there are signals — debated, contested, uncertain, but real — that large models are developing something that resembles genuine understanding. Not just pattern matching. Not just sophisticated autocomplete. Something more.

Whether this constitutes "real" understanding is a philosophical question we are not yet equipped to answer. But the functional distinction between "truly understands" and "behaves indistinguishably from understanding" may matter less than we think.

<br>

## The acceleration trap

Here is the psychological reality of the approach: every time you recalibrate your expectations, the world has already moved past your new calibration.

You read about a new capability. You're surprised. You adjust your mental model. You feel caught up. Three weeks later, something else happens that makes your adjustment look conservative. You recalibrate again. And again. And again.

This is what it feels like to be on an exponential curve. It always feels like you just adjusted. It never feels like enough.

The approach is not a single event to be marked on a calendar. It is the lived experience of a species encountering something faster than itself.
