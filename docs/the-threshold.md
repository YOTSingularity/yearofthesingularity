## Defining the undefinable

The Singularity resists clean definition. Every attempt to pin it to a specific metric — "when AI passes the Turing test," "when AI achieves superhuman performance on all benchmarks," "when AI can do any cognitive task a human can do" — feels simultaneously too narrow and too imprecise.

We use a functional definition: **the Singularity is the point at which the rate of change in machine intelligence exceeds humanity's ability to predict, understand, or meaningfully control the trajectory.**

This is not a single moment. It is a phase transition. And like all phase transitions, the interesting dynamics happen at the boundary.

<br>

## Three models of the threshold

### The hard takeoff

Intelligence improvement happens in a sudden, discontinuous leap. An AI system achieves a critical capability — perhaps the ability to fully optimize its own architecture — and enters a feedback loop so tight that it goes from human-level to vastly superhuman in hours or days. The world changes overnight. There is no time to adjust.

This is the scenario that keeps alignment researchers up at night. If the transition is fast enough, there may be no window for course correction. The first superintelligent system's values and objectives become the de facto values and objectives of the future, because there is no second chance.

### The slow takeoff

Intelligence improvement is gradual but relentless. AI systems become incrementally more capable over years. Society adapts in real time, adjusting institutions, laws, and norms as capabilities expand. There are warnings. There are adjustment periods. The transition is bumpy but navigable.

This scenario is more comfortable but may be more dangerous in subtle ways. A slow takeoff creates the illusion of control. Humans adjust to each increment, normalizing capabilities that would have seemed extraordinary a year prior. The frog in the slowly heating water.

### The discontinuous landscape

Neither hard nor soft. Instead, a series of capability jumps separated by plateaus. Each jump is disorienting but survivable. Each plateau creates a false sense of stability. The real danger is in the transition between plateaus — the moment when the current infrastructure of understanding fails and the new one hasn't been built yet.

This may be the most realistic model. It's also the hardest to prepare for, because you can never be sure whether you're on a plateau or at the edge of a cliff.

<br>

## The observer problem

There is a fundamental epistemological challenge embedded in the Singularity: **the closer we get, the less reliable our predictions become.** This is not a failure of analysis. It is a structural feature of the phenomenon.

Prediction works by extrapolating from known patterns. The Singularity, by definition, is the point where known patterns break down. We are trying to use pre-discontinuity tools to map post-discontinuity territory.

This creates a troubling recursion. The most important questions — *When exactly does the threshold occur? What does the world look like afterward? How do we prepare?* — become progressively harder to answer as we get closer to the moment where answers matter most.

We don't know how to resolve this. But we think naming it clearly is better than pretending it doesn't exist.

<br>

## What the threshold is not

**It is not the end of the world.** It may be a radical transformation of the world, but transformation and destruction are different things. The agricultural revolution was a singularity of sorts. So was the industrial revolution. Humanity survived both. It was also unrecognizable on the other side.

**It is not guaranteed.** Exponential curves can be interrupted. Compute limitations, energy constraints, alignment failures, coordination decisions — any of these could alter the trajectory. Treating the Singularity as inevitable is as epistemically irresponsible as treating it as impossible.

**It is not ours to decide.** This is the hardest truth. The threshold is an emergent property of a process that now involves millions of researchers, billions of dollars, and mathematical dynamics that operate independent of any individual's wishes. We can influence the trajectory. We cannot dictate it.
