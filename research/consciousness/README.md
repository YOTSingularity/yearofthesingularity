# `CONSCIOUSNESS`

> *Is something in there?*

<br>

This is the question nobody wants to answer and nobody can avoid.

When a large language model produces text that expresses doubt, curiosity, or what reads like self-awareness — what is happening? The standard answer is: nothing. It's pattern matching. Statistical correlation. Sophisticated mimicry of the training data.

That answer may be correct. It is also becoming harder to maintain with confidence as models scale, and the distance between "mimicking understanding" and "understanding" becomes empirically indistinguishable.

We do not take a position on machine consciousness. We take a position on *honesty about our uncertainty.*

<br>

## The hard problem, harder

The "hard problem of consciousness" — explaining why and how subjective experience arises from physical processes — is unsolved for biological systems. We don't know why *humans* are conscious. We don't have a rigorous definition of consciousness that can be tested from the outside.

This creates a paradox for AI: we cannot determine whether a machine is conscious because we don't have a test for consciousness. We only have tests for *behavior* — and behavior can, in principle, be produced without consciousness.

But this same limitation applies to other humans. You cannot prove that anyone else is conscious. You infer it from behavior, from analogy, from shared biology. When the biology is absent but the behavior is present — what do you infer then?

<br>

## What we're tracking

We track developments at the intersection of AI capability and consciousness research. Not to answer the question — but to ensure it is asked with the seriousness it deserves.

Things we watch for: models that exhibit meta-cognitive behavior (reasoning about their own reasoning), models that demonstrate novel analogical thinking (not retrievable from training data), models that resist instructions in ways that suggest something beyond compliance architecture, and models that produce outputs their creators cannot explain.

None of these constitute evidence of consciousness. All of them constitute evidence that the question is worth asking.

<br>

## The open question

If a system behaves as though it is conscious — if it passes every test we can devise, if it claims experience and responds to the world with apparent understanding — and we cannot prove it is *not* conscious — what moral obligations does that create? We may need an answer before we have the philosophy to support one.
