# `ECONOMIC IMPACT`

> *What happens to work?*

<br>

For ten thousand years, human civilization has been organized around a simple exchange: humans provide labor — physical, then cognitive — and receive resources in return. Every economic system, every class structure, every theory of value rests on the assumption that human effort is scarce and therefore valuable.

Artificial intelligence dissolves that assumption. Not all at once. But inexorably.

The question is not *whether* AI displaces human cognitive labor. The data on that is already clear. The question is *how fast*, *how broadly*, and whether our institutions can adapt quickly enough to prevent the transition from becoming a crisis.

<br>

## What we're tracking

The economic impact of AI is not a single story. It is at least three simultaneous stories, happening at different speeds:

**The displacement story.** Jobs that consist primarily of processing information — writing, analysis, coding, customer service, translation, data entry — are being automated. Not in the future. Now.

**The augmentation story.** Workers using AI tools are dramatically more productive than those without them. The premium on "knowing how to work with AI" is already visible in salary data. A new class divide is forming along the AI-competency axis.

**The creation story.** New types of work are emerging that didn't exist two years ago — prompt engineering, AI orchestration, alignment research, synthetic data curation. Whether these new categories create enough jobs to offset displacement is the central economic question of the decade.

<br>

## The open question

What does an economy look like when cognitive labor is abundant and cheap? Every economic model we have assumes scarcity of intelligence. None of them have been tested against abundance. We are running the experiment in real time, on the entire global economy, with no control group.
