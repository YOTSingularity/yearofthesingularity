# `EMERGENCE`

> *Capabilities nobody designed.*

<br>

Perhaps the most unsettling finding in modern AI: when you make a model big enough, it starts doing things you never asked it to do. Arithmetic. Logical reasoning. Translation between languages it was barely exposed to. Theory of mind. These capabilities were not programmed. They were not trained for. They *emerged.*

The implication is profound: we do not fully understand what we are building. We can predict aggregate performance improvements. We cannot predict which specific new abilities will appear at the next scale threshold. We are surprised every time. And we are running out of room for surprise.

<br>

## Key papers

### [Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.07682)
Wei et al., 2022

Why it matters: The first systematic documentation of emergence — capabilities that appear suddenly at scale thresholds rather than improving gradually. Forced the field to confront the fact that capability gains are not always smooth or predictable.

### [Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.15004)
Schaeffer et al., 2023

Why it matters: Challenged the emergence narrative, arguing that some "sudden" capability gains are artifacts of measurement choices. A crucial counter-signal that keeps our understanding honest. The debate is not settled.

<br>

## The open question

If emergence is real, it means that every future model at sufficient scale may develop capabilities we cannot anticipate. We are building systems that surprise their creators. How do you align something you can't predict?
