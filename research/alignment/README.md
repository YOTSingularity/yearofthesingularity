# `ALIGNMENT`

> *Can we control what we can't outthink?*

<br>

Alignment is the problem of ensuring that AI systems do what we want them to do — and only what we want them to do — even as they become more capable than we are. It is the most important unsolved problem in the history of engineering.

The difficulty is not technical in the conventional sense. It is conceptual. We struggle to formally specify what we want, even to ourselves. Human values are contradictory, contextual, and evolving. Translating them into mathematical objectives that remain robust at superhuman capability levels is a challenge that may be harder than building the superintelligence itself.

<br>

## The core tensions

**Capability vs. control.** The more capable a system is, the more difficult it is to constrain. A system smart enough to solve cancer is also smart enough to find loopholes in its constraints. This is not a bug — it is a logical consequence of intelligence.

**Specification vs. intent.** We can specify objectives precisely. We cannot specify *what we actually mean* precisely. The gap between the specification and the intent — Goodhart's Law applied to superintelligence — is where catastrophic misalignment lives.

**Speed vs. safety.** Every day spent on alignment is a day the capability clock ticks forward without matching safety progress. Every day spent rushing capabilities is a day the alignment gap widens. There is no solution to this tension. There is only management of it.

<br>

## The open question

Is alignment solvable in principle? Or is "controlling a system smarter than you" a logical contradiction — like trying to build a box that something smarter than you can't escape? The optimists point to mathematical frameworks. The pessimists point to the impossibility results. The honest position is that we don't know. And the clock is ticking.
