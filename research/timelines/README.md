# `TIMELINES`

> *Who predicts what, and why.*

<br>

Prediction is hard. Prediction about exponential processes is harder. Prediction about exponential processes that affect the predictor is the hardest thing there is.

Every serious prediction about AI timelines made in the last two decades has shared a common failure mode: **underestimation.** Not by small margins — by years and sometimes decades. This consistent directional error is itself a data point worth studying.

<br>

## The prediction record

A brief, humbling history:

**2015** — Median expert prediction for "AI that can write a high-school essay": **2025-2030.** Actual: 2022.

**2016** — Median expert prediction for "AI that beats humans at all Atari games": **2025.** Actual: 2020.

**2017** — Median expert prediction for "AI that can translate at professional level": **2030.** Actual: 2023.

**2019** — Median expert prediction for "AI that can pass the bar exam": **2035-2040.** Actual: 2023.

The pattern is consistent and directional. Experts — the people with the most information and the most relevant expertise — systematically underestimate the pace.

<br>

## Why predictions fail

**Anchoring to linear intuition.** Human brains expect linear progress. Exponential progress looks like nothing, then looks like everything, with almost no transition between the two. We consistently underestimate the "everything" phase because our intuitions were calibrated during the "nothing" phase.

**Ignoring compound effects.** Each capability advance enables the next. Progress is not a series of independent events — it's a cascade. Predicting any single advance correctly doesn't help if you miss the compound dynamics.

**Social anchoring.** Predictions cluster because humans anchor to each other. If the consensus is "2030," it takes unusual conviction to say "2025." The result is a consensus prediction that systematically lags reality.

<br>

## Current landscape

As of 2026, expert predictions for AGI range from "already achieved" to "2040+" to "never." The distribution has widened, not narrowed, as the approach has accelerated. This is itself informative: it suggests we are in a regime where even experts cannot agree on where we are relative to the threshold.

<br>

## The open question

At what point do predictions become meaningless? If the pace of change exceeds the speed of prediction, we enter a regime where forecasting is structurally impossible — not because of ignorance, but because the system evolves faster than any model of it. We may already be there.
