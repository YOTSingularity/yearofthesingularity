# `SCALING LAWS`

> *The mathematics of more.*

<br>

The discovery that AI capability scales predictably with compute, data, and parameters is arguably the most important empirical finding in the field. It transforms AI progress from an unpredictable series of breakthroughs into a *forecastable engineering trajectory.*

This is both reassuring and terrifying. Reassuring because predictability enables planning. Terrifying because the predictions point toward capability levels we are not prepared for.

<br>

## Key papers

### [Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.08361)
Kaplan et al., 2020

Why it matters: Established that model performance follows smooth power laws across seven orders of magnitude. This paper turned "bigger is better" from a hunch into a law, and launched the scaling era.

### [Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.15556)
Hoffmann et al., 2022 (Chinchilla)

Why it matters: Demonstrated that most large models were undertrained relative to their size. Shifted the paradigm from "make it bigger" to "make it more efficient," paradoxically accelerating progress by showing how much capability was being left on the table.

### [Scaling Data-Constrained Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16264)
Muennighoff et al., 2023

Why it matters: Addressed the data wall. If we run out of training data, do scaling laws break? The answer appears to be: not as badly as feared, but the constraint is real. This paper maps the boundary of the current paradigm.

<br>

## The open question

Scaling laws tell us *how much* capability improves with resources. They do not tell us *what capabilities* emerge at which thresholds. The predictability of the aggregate masks the unpredictability of the specific. This is the gap where surprises live.
